After the IIWW, the population of Venice peaked, with more than 174,800 people living in the island.

The island was one of the centers of Italian culture, society, and economy.

With the industrial development of the inlands and the increased working opportunities, people began leaving the island.

By 1978, less then 100,000 people lived in Venice.

Venice is disappearing, and we don't know what to do about it.

It was early morning on April 16, 2023, when the serpentine alleys of Venice echoed with a chorus of voices. About a thousand Venetias rallied together to raise awareness about the ongoing depopulation plaguing their island home. They called for improved wages, fair housing costs, and job opportunities for a sustainable life in this ancient city.

Venice, with its centuries-old architecture, has long been fighting a battle of survival against rising sea levels and subsidence. Today, however, the city finds itself wrestling with an equally urgent issue: a dwindling native population. A worrying trend reflective of Italy's aging demographic and lower birth rates.

While Venetians packed their bags, tourists flooded in, tuning this city into an international vacation hotspot. Since the close of World War II, the charm of Venice's canals and squares attracted vacationers from around the globe, bringing big influxes of foreign currencies as well as a number of problems for locals.

Excessive numbers of tourists overwhelmed the city's infrastructure and resources. On some days, tourists vastly outnumber residents. The influx of tourists also increased the demand for goods and services, which resulted in a rise in prices. This tourist-led inflation impacts the locals significantly as they found the cost of living becoming increasingly unaffordable. Also, the city's reliance on tourism has led to a lack of diversity in job opportunities. Much of the employment in Venice is tied to tourism, and can be precarious, seasonal, and often low-paying.
