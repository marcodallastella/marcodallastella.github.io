The reovolution of polyurethan swimsuits

“Here, take this and see if that really works!” my swim coach told me one day.

He threw at me something a swimsuit unlike any I had ever seen in real life. It covered the entirely of legs and the whole upper body. To my eyes, it resembled something like women would wear. Wearing was a long and laborious process. Once wore, it adhered like a second skin. Out of the water it constrained my movements and the though of having to swim four kilometers with that thing on seemed unconceivable. I reluctantly put that new swimsuit on. I didn’t notice any change immediately Lap after lap, my coach started taking times with his stopwatch and noting them in his notebook. Eventually, the evidence was clear. I was going faster. That was the beginning of the revolution of tech swimsuits. In the early 2000s, Olypic athletes started using them and the trend trickled down to the whole swimming community. By 2007, it was unconceivable to compete in a race without one of those things on.

The tech-swimsuit era was inaugurated at the Sydney Olympics and no other athlete better exemplified this trend Ian Thorpe, the australian thorpedo (that was his nickname) who raced with a full body suit covering his whole body, from the wrists to his ankles. He was the one touching the wall before the US champion and recordman Gary Hall Jr, who before the race wrote on his blog that the US relay was going to smash the aussies like guitars.

But that was only the beginnig. While those swimsuits gave a noticeable contribution to the lowering of average times in the sport, it wasn’t until 2008 that this technologically advancement really started become a revolution.

The Speedo LZR was unveiled in New York City in February 2008. That marked the introduction of a new material, polyurethan. At the Beijing Summer Olympics, the Speedo LZR proved its possibility setting 25 new world records and 65 new Olympic records. With that swimsuit on, an already legendary swimmer like Michael Phelps won eight gold medals. But it was really the Rome Olympics that convinced the World Aquatics (then FINA) to change the rules. In rome, 43 world records were set. This convinced FINA to change the regulations to ban any polyurethane in swimsuits.
