After more than ten years, tech swimsuits are a thing of the past.

Marco Dalla Stella

“Here, take this and see if it works for you!” exclaimed my swim coach one day, casually tossing a swimsuit unlike anything I had ever seen before. It covered my entire body, from ankles to chest, bearing an uncanny resemblance to women's swimwear upon first glance.

Putting it on was a lengthy and arduous process. Once I wore it, it clung to my body like a second skin. Out of the water, it restricted my movements, and the thought of swimming two miles with that thing on seemed unfathomable.

Reclutantly, gave the new swimsuit a try. At first, I didn’t notice any immediate changes. Lap after lap, my coach timed me meticulously, noting down the results in his notebook. Eventually, the evidence was clear: I was swimming faster.

In the late 1990s, a wave of technological swept through competitive swimming, leading to the creation of swimsuits engineered to enhance buoyancy and stremline movement. These innovative suits were carefully engineered using wind tunnels testing.

The grand stage for these pioneering swimsuits arrived with the 2000 Sydney Olimpics, where Ian Thorpe dominated the 400m freestyle while wearing a full-body suit that cloaked him from wrist to ankle. Among the finalists, only Romanian swimmer Dragos Coman wore a traditional slip swimsuit.

But the true revolution occurred a few years later, in February 2008, when Speedo unveiled the LZR Racer Suit in New York. This high-tech swimwear utilized polyurethane fabric, meticulously tested in NASA's wind tunnels and bolstered by advanced flow analysis software.

The Speedo LZR proved its superiority at the 2008 Beijing Olympics, where it propelled athletes smash an incredible 25 world records and 65 Olympic records. Other brands adapted, and hastened to develop their own polyurethan swimsuits. The trend continued in 2009 at the World Championships in Rome, where a a jaw-dropping 43 world records were set.

A look at the history of swimming's world records provides a glimpse into the revolution that unfolded.

In just a couple of years, more world records were set than in the previous seven. It comes as no suprise that in 2009, FINA (today known as World Aquatics), the international governing body of swimming, introduced a ban on non-textile swimsuits, starting from the year 2010.

While FINA's ban brought an end to the era of record-breaking swimsuits, it left existing records untouched, leaving the swimming community pondering n intriguing question: When will the sport catch up with its own evolution? Many believed that these seemingly invincible records would have lasted forever.

Fast forward more than a decade, only eight world records remain unbroken from the frenzy of the tech swimsuit era. Yet, a new generation of extraordinary swimmers has emerged, led by the likes of David Popovici, Thomas Ceccon, and Ariarne Titmus. With each stroke, they inch ever closer to erasing even these longstanding records.
