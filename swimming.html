“Here, take this and see if it works for you!” my swim coach told me one day, tossing a swimsuit unlike anything I had ever seen before. It covered my entire body, from my ankles to my chest, and to my eyes it resembled a swimsuit for women.

Putting it on was a lengthy and arduous process. Once I wore it, it clung to my body like a second skin. Out of the water, it restricted my movements, and the thought of swimming two miles with that thing on seemed unimaginable.

Reclutantly, I donned the new swimsuit. At first, I didn’t notice any immediate changes. Lap after lap, my coach started timing me, anxiously recording the results in his notebook. The evidence was clear: I was going faster.

In the late Nineties, technological innovations introduced new swimsuits designed to increase beauyoancy and sliding. These swimsuits were designed in wind tunnels, with exceptional investment of engineering knowledge.

These new swimsuits made their debut on the bigger stage at the 2000 Sydney Olimpics, and no other athlete better exemplies this first revolution than Ian Thorpe, who raced – and won – win a full-body suit that covered him from wrists to ankles. Of the eight participants to the final of the 400 freestyle only Romanian swimmer Dragos Coman wore an old-fashioned slip swimsuit.

But the real revolution occurred a few years later, when in February 2008, Speedo presendet in New York the LZR Racer Suit, a new high-technology line of swimsuits that introduced polyurethane in the fabric, tested in NASA’s wind tunnel testing facilities and with the support of flow analysis software.

At the 2008 Beijing Olympics, the Speedo LZR proved its superiority by helping athlete to set an unprecedente 25 World Records and 65 Olympic records. Other brands developed their own polyurethane swimsuits, and the following year at the 2009 World Championships held in Rome, a stunning 43 world records were set.

More than ten years laters, only eight world records are still resisting from the crazy two years of the tech swimsuits era. But a new generation of exceptional swimmers – such as David Popovici, Thomas Ceccon, Ariarne Titmus – is getting increasingly close to erase even those.

If we look at how times has evolved over the years, it seems clear that very soon the era of non-textile swimming suit will be only a memory of the past.
