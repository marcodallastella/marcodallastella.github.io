After more than a decade, the swimming technological revolution is a thing of the past.

“Here, take this and see if it really works!” my swim coach told me one day, tossing a swimsuit unlike anything I had ever seen before. It covered my entire body, from my legs to my upper body, and at first glance, it resembled something a woman would wear.

Puttig it on was a lengthy and arduous process. Once I wore it, it clung to my body like a second skin. Out of the water, it restricted my movements, and the thought of swimming four kilometers with that thing on seemed unimaginable.

Reluctanlty, I donned the new swimsuit. At first, I didn't notice any immediate changes. Lap after lap, my coach started timing me with his stopwatch and recording the results in his notebook. Eventually, the evidence became clear: I was going faster.

That was the beginning of the tech swimsuit revolution. In the early 2000s, Olympic athletes started using them, and the trend spread throughout the entire swimming community. By 2007, competing in a race without one of these suits was inconceivable.

But tech-swimsuit era has a year zero, at the 2000 Sydney Olympics, and no athlete better exemplified this trend than Ian Thorpe, the Australian "Thorpedo" (as he was nicknamed), who raced with a full-body suit that covered him from wrists to ankles. In the 4x100 freestyle relay, he was the first to touch the wall, beating the US champion and record-holder Gary Hall Jr., who had confidently predicted on his blog that the US team would "smash the Aussies like guitars."

But that was only the beginning. While these swimsuits did contribute to make those swimmers go faster, it wasn't until 2008 that what seemed like a normal technological advancement became revolution.

The Speedo LZR was unveiled in New York City in February 2008. That marked the introduction of a new material, polyurethan. At the Beijing Summer Olympics, the Speedo LZR proved its possibility setting 25 new world records and 65 new Olympic records. With that swimsuit on, an already legendary swimmer like Michael Phelps won eight gold medals. But it was really the Rome Olympics that convinced the World Aquatics (then FINA) to change the rules. In rome, 43 world records were set. This convinced FINA to change the regulations to ban any polyurethane in swimsuits.
